H2 - Our Cabinet Refinishing Process
Every successful cabinet project begins with a disciplined process. Cabinets are not walls. They receive
constant handling, exposure to grease, humidity, food residue, and repeated cleaning. Because of that, the
preparation and coating steps must be more controlled than a standard interior paint project. A professional
refinishing workflow starts with inspection and planning. Cabinet doors and drawer fronts are removed, labeled,
and organized so every component returns to the right opening. Hinges, knobs, and pulls are also tracked
carefully to keep reinstallation efficient and clean. Once the doors are off, the cabinet surfaces are degreased.
This step matters more than many homeowners realize. Kitchens collect invisible residue over time, especially

near cooking areas, ranges, islands, and sink walls. If that residue is left behind, even premium coatings can
fail. After cleaning, the surfaces are sanded and deglossed to reduce sheen, smooth minor defects, and create
a profile the primer can bond to correctly.
• Door removal and labeling for organized reinstallation
• Surface degreasing to remove residue and improve adhesion
• Sanding and deglossing to prepare the existing finish
• Minor wood repair and surface refinement
• Bonding primer application for long-term performance
• Professional spray finishing for a smooth, factory-like look

After the initial prep, the detail work begins. Small dents, scratches, edge wear, nail holes, and minor surface
inconsistencies are filled and refined. Hardware holes can be corrected when alignment needs to be improved,
and cabinet doors can be adjusted so the finished presentation looks straighter and more custom. Only after
that detail work is complete does the primer stage begin. Primer is not a technicality; it is one of the foundations
of durability. A cabinet-grade bonding primer creates a stable base between the old surface and the new finish,
helping block bleed-through and supporting long-term adhesion. With the primer cured and lightly sanded for
smoothness, the finish coats are applied. Professional spray application is usually the preferred method
because it creates a far more uniform appearance than brushing or rolling. Spray finishes level out better,
produce cleaner edges, and eliminate heavy texture. That is what gives refinished cabinetry the smoother,
factory-like appearance homeowners expect when they invest in this type of upgrade.
H2 - Surface Preparation & Detail Work
Preparation is where the quality of the finished project is decided. If edges are rough, grease is left on the
substrate, or glossy surfaces are not conditioned properly, the coating system is already compromised before
the color even goes on. A disciplined preparation phase improves how the finish looks on day one and how it
performs months later. It also allows the doors, drawer fronts, and frames to read as one cohesive set instead
of separate components that were painted at different levels of quality. That is why this stage should be
described clearly on the page. Homeowners may not know the technical names for deglossing, filler work, or
bonding primer, but they do understand durability and smoothness. The landing should connect the
preparation steps to those outcomes directly: better adhesion, cleaner lines, fewer imperfections, and a more
polished result. Preparation also affects how the final color reads in the room. A cabinet face with poor sanding
or uneven patching will catch light differently than a properly prepared surface. That creates visual
inconsistency that becomes more obvious in bright kitchens. By explaining the prep stage in practical terms,
the landing helps homeowners understand why refinishing is a craft process and not just a color change.

H2 - Refinishing vs. Replacement
Many homeowners compare refinishing with refacing or full replacement. A well-structured landing should help
clarify that difference. Full replacement removes the existing cabinetry entirely and usually carries the highest
price, longest timeline, and most disruption. Refacing changes the visible skins and doors while keeping the
cabinet boxes. Refinishing keeps the overall cabinet structure and updates the finish system, which can be the

most efficient path when the cabinets are still fundamentally sound. That does not mean refinishing is right for
every kitchen. If cabinets are failing structurally or the layout no longer works, a larger remodel may be justified.
But for many properties, especially those with solid cabinet boxes and a workable kitchen plan, refinishing
gives the homeowner the best ratio of cost, speed, and visual impact. Including that perspective on the page
adds depth and makes the content feel more credible.

are still usable but the finish and look are outdated.
